// Your starting position

You have three advantages most people don't

This isn't motivation-poster talk. These are real, structural advantages in the job market you're aiming at.

🇺🇸

You're a US citizen

Being Puerto Rican means you're a US citizen, and that makes you eligible for a security clearance. Most people on Earth can never get one. Cleared IT workers earn more and get hired faster, because the clearance is often harder to find than the skills.

🪖

You're already on base

You work inside a US military installation. The IT jobs there (government and contractor) hire from people who are already local, already badged, already known. You can literally walk to the offices that will hire you.

🎓

DoD doesn't need your diploma

Department of Defense rules (called DoD 8140) qualify people for IT jobs based on certifications, not degrees. A $400 exam you can pass with free YouTube training counts. A 4-year degree is optional in this world.

THE CHEAT CODE

CompTIA Security+ is the key that opens the base doors

Almost every IT job touching DoD networks legally requires a baseline certification, and Security+ is the standard one. Help desk at the base network center, contractor field tech, sysadmin support: they all list it. That's why this whole roadmap points at Security+ by the end of year one. Once you hold it and can get a clearance, you are in a small pool of people that government contractors compete to hire.

// The route

Shift through the gears 🏁

One year, five gears. Each gear is a milestone with a clear finish line. Don't skip gears, but don't stay parked either. The timeline assumes you study 5 to 8 hours a week while keeping your job.

1st
Months 0 – 2 · Free

Test drive: find out if you like this

Before spending a cent, put in a few weeks and see if this world clicks for you.

  • Watch Professor Messer's free CompTIA A+ course on YouTube. It's the standard free training, and it's genuinely good.
  • Get hands-on: open up an old PC, reinstall Windows on something, mess with your phone's settings, break stuff and fix it.
  • Optional paid shortcut: the Google IT Support Certificate on Coursera (about $49/month, most finish in 3 to 5 months). Nice resume line, not required.
Cost: $0
2nd
Months 2 – 6

CompTIA A+ · your first real cert

A+ proves you can support computers, printers, phones, and users. It's the standard entry ticket for help desk jobs everywhere.

  • Two exams (Core 1 and Core 2), roughly $250+ each. Check current prices at comptia.org.
  • Study: Professor Messer (free) + practice exams (ExamCompass is free, or Messer's paid practice tests).
  • Start applying to entry help desk jobs the day you pass. Don't wait until you feel "ready." Nobody ever does.
Cost: ~$500 in exams
3rd
Months 6 – 9 · Optional but smart

CompTIA Network+ · learn how it all connects

Networking is the plumbing of IT. Understanding it makes every job after this easier.

  • One exam, roughly $370.
  • Honest note: if money is tight, you can skip straight to Security+. Network+ makes Security+ easier and looks good on a resume, but Security+ is the one employers require.
Cost: ~$370 (skippable)
4th
Months 9 – 12 · The big one

CompTIA Security+ · the DoD door opener

This is the cert this whole plan is built around. One exam, roughly $400, and it satisfies the DoD 8140 baseline that base IT jobs demand.

  • Study: Professor Messer's free Security+ course + practice exams until you score 85%+ consistently.
  • The moment you pass: make a USAJOBS.gov account and search IT jobs at your installation, and check contractor career pages (GDIT, Leidos, Vectrus, KBR all staff bases in Germany).
  • When a job offers to sponsor your security clearance, take it, even if the pay is average. The clearance is worth more than the first salary.
Cost: ~$400
5th
Year 2+ · Cruising speed

Pick a lane and specialize

By now you're working in IT and getting paid to learn. Year two is where you pick a specialty track (below) and your salary starts climbing seriously. This is also where remote work opens up.

Employer often pays for these certs
// Year 2 and beyond

Three lanes. All of them pay. Pick what pulls you.

You don't have to decide today. You'll know by the time you get there, because you'll have been working around all three.

Defend networks for a living

CySA+SSCPlater: CISSP

The natural next step after Security+. You'd work in a Security Operations Center (SOC) watching for and responding to attacks, or as a security specialist on base. Best fit with a clearance: cleared cyber roles are among the best-paid jobs that don't need a degree, and DoD has a permanent shortage.

Remote-friendly: SOC analyst roles are commonly remote in the US market.

Why it's AI-proof

AI makes attacks cheaper and more common, which means more defense jobs, not fewer. Companies need humans who are accountable for security decisions. The tooling changes; the need grows.

Run the infrastructure everything lives on

AWS Cloud PractitionerAWS Solutions Architect Assoc.or Azure AZ-104

Everything runs in the cloud now, including the military (they're huge Azure customers). Cloud admins and cloud support engineers manage servers that live in data centers, which means the work is remote by nature. This is the most direct route to a work-from-anywhere career.

Start with AWS Cloud Practitioner or Azure AZ-900 (both beginner-level, ~$100 exams) to taste it.

Why it's AI-proof

Every AI product runs on cloud infrastructure. The AI boom is literally a cloud spending boom. Someone has to build, secure, and babysit all of it.

Be the person who makes AI actually work

Azure AI-900Python basicsAI tool fluency

This one is less a cert path and more a multiplier on the other two. Learn to use AI tools seriously (automating tasks, writing scripts with AI help, building small workflows), add basic Python, and you become the tech on the team who gets 3x more done. Small businesses everywhere need "the person who sets up AI for us," and almost nobody without a degree is competing for that yet.

The honest truth about AI and jobs

AI is killing the boring half of IT (password resets, ticket triage). It is not killing hands-on hardware work, security, infrastructure, or people who wield AI well. Position yourself on the winning side of that line from day one.

"AI won't take your job. A person using AI will. So be that person."
